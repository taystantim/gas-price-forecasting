Introduction

This project analyzes weekly New York Harbor gasoline prices over 40 years using time series methods. The goal is to model both price dynamics and volatility using ARIMA and GARCH approaches.

Weekly New York gasoline prices from 1986 to 2026 show a clear long-term upward trend, rising from around 0.40c per gallon in the late 1980s to over $2.00 per gallon in recent years. However, the series also exhibits substantial fluctuations, with sharp spikes and declines reflecting major economic and energy market shocks. This combination of long-term growth and short-term volatility highlights the dynamic and unpredictable nature of gasoline prices over time.

The ACF plot shows very slow decay and strong autocorrelation across many lags, indicating that the log gasoline price series is likely non-stationary. The Augmented Dickey–Fuller test fails to reject the unit root at the 5% level (p = 0.095), suggesting the series is non-stationary and requires differencing before modeling.

After first differencing the log prices, the ACF and PACF show much weaker autocorrelation with most lags within the confidence bands, indicating the series is now stationary. The Augmented Dickey–Fuller test strongly rejects the unit root hypothesis (p = 0.01), confirming that the differenced log series is stationary and suitable for ARIMA modeling.

The ARIMA model produced an RMSE of 0.0468, which is nearly identical to the RMSE of a naive random walk forecast (0.0469). This indicates that while the ARIMA model captures the serial dependence structure of the data, it provides little improvement over a simple random walk for short-term forecasting. Such results are common in financial and commodity price series, which often behave close to random walks.

Conclusion

This project analyzed weekly New York gasoline prices from 1986 to 2026 using time series methods. The price series was found to be non-stationary, while the log returns were stationary, allowing the mean dynamics to be modeled using an ARIMA(4,1,1) model. Forecast validation showed that gasoline prices behave similarly to a random walk, meaning future price levels tend to remain near the most recent value and are difficult to predict accurately.

Volatility analysis revealed clear volatility clustering, confirmed by the ARCH LM test. A GARCH(1,1) model was therefore used to capture time-varying variance. The results showed that volatility shocks are persistent, with a half-life of roughly eight weeks, meaning the effects of large price movements fade gradually over time. Major economic events, including the 2008 financial crisis, COVID-19 pandemic, and 2022 energy shock, correspond to significant spikes in volatility.

Overall, the results suggest that while the direction of gasoline prices is largely unpredictable, their volatility follows persistent and predictable patterns, making ARIMA–GARCH models useful for understanding energy price dynamics.
